The design process at its core. Some details may vary according to specific methods and when the type of proposal is known in advance.

1 â€“ Brief

Formulating the problem. We select the context, make the first observation, and then write down a creative brief, some research questions, and hypotheses to validate.

2 â€“ Discover

Getting into a broad and exhaustive research. In this stage we are like kids. We question everything and empathize with everyone. We learn a lot, and it feels very nice.

3 â€“ Define

Looking into the details. This is about understanding deeply reasons, intentions, beliefs, and motivations. Then we specify insights and write the brief as a premise, a point-of-view.

Making it real. Time to share the feeling with the people. And also, we evaluate doubts that may be seeds for new projects, because, at the end of the day, we are lifelong learners.

Design Tools

Some non-technical tools we use to yield critical thinking during the design process.
Observation
Observation

Storytelling is an important tool throughout the entire design process and may be applied in several ways. For instance, starting from the observation phase, we may ask the users to narrate an event or to describe what they are thinking and doing while they are in a specific task. This way, they become more conscious of what they might be doing by custom and we discover hidden concerns or motivations.

Literally, what does a fly on the wall do? Observe without interfering.

When talking to the user, sometimes he may tell us some lies or bias his answers, consciously or unconsciously. So setting up a hidden camera is very useful to see what people actually do and compare it with what they say they do.

Tracking down an entire day of the user cannot give us representative information on his values. Nevertheless, one single day becomes quite precious when analyzing his activities because they are not carried out in the same way ever, even if they are part of a routine. A day may reveal us a plethora of unanticipated issues that our proposal will need to solve.

Shadowing is a handy tool to empathize with the user because as we track down in detail his day-to-day interactions, we are able to immerse in his routines as if we were the actual user. Besides, from a viewer perspective, shadowing may also reveal us some design opportunities when capturing the function of the objects as a complement for the user's behavior.

Self-Recording is a golden mean between a hidden camera and a live conversation with the user. It is about giving him freedom to express his own way but also in a detailed way.

By asking the user to self-record himself, he teaches us how to perform a certain activity from his point of view, and we capture some valuable findings on his way of talking and acting.

Interviewing is a way of getting specific information on the user. We conduct interviews with one or two persons in the context and may involve more participants as they come to listen or support the interviewed. Besides, even though we prepare a list of key questions, we are agile to build new ones as the old ones become seeds of conversation among the participants.

This is a handful tool both for observation and prototyping. The aim is to get feedback from contacts or social media communities from different places around the world so that we can relate our research to international design principles and keep in mind diverse contexts.

In a conversation, often answering with more questions is useful to get deeper answers. Five Whys? is a simple and efficient way to do it.

It is about leading the user to be authentic by reducing the time to think, and by asking "Why" the user may be forced to express the underlying reasons for his behaviors and attitudes.

Analysis
Analysis

An actors map is a representation that let us identify, analyze, and communicate dynamics between the people involved in a service or experience.

The main utility we get here is knowing who are the most influential actors in a situation and the roles and responsibilities of each one.

An interaction map is a useful way to understand what makes up a practice. It is about looking through media recordings of an observation and listing Activities, Environments, Interactions, Objects, and Users. Then, we find connections and realize values and beliefs that might have given rise to the practice.

Looking at the history of an industry, organization, group, market segment, or practice, helps us get a thoughtful understanding of why it is as is.

Particularly, through a historical analysis we can spot key events that gave birth to a specific value and find patterns that may become future trends across other contexts.

Sometimes an observation in context may be limited or not representative enough to make conclusions, then complementing it with data
on the population is pretty useful.

An anthropometric analysis allows us to determine how relevant is an insight and to have an idea of how much coverage our proposal may have if we decide to go on with a certain finding.

In the first observations we focus more on quantity than quality not to underestimate any curiosity. However, when analyzing, we must start selecting high-quality data and real insights.

The affinity diagrams let us select the most relevant findings by determining common denominators and clustering according to similarity, dependence, etc.

We talk about 4 flows of capital: intellectual capital â€“knowledge and informationâ€“, social capital and relationships, matter and energy,
and economic capital.

Determining flows and their connections are a convenient way to understand relations with the environment in a systemic approach and perceive spotlights on the people's activities.

Although this is a method for the observation stage, making a secondary â€“or deskâ€“ research is also very helpful to analyze existent findings on the context or the state of the art.

We often rely on some articles, papers, magazines, opinions from experts, to have an informed point of view and avoid judging from our own experiences.

Forecasting is writing texts or recording videos about what a possible, plausible, probable, and preferable future may look like.

Here it is important to note that forecasts are not judgments but a reflection on the implications of the behaviors, technology, and design decisions, from our perspective as human beings and our common social values.
